Organizing Information

Most of my front-end work has been in fairly densely related data, and the presentation concerns that come with it. When every piece of information is two degrees of separation from thousands of others, the biggest challenge is deciding what stays and what goes. Why the person is looking at the application should dictate the organization of the data. What should be front and center, and what they will drill down to see and what they might only care about once in a blue moon. This requires a sense of organization, and a lot of thinking about thinking.

Lots of factors go into seemingly simple displays of data. Position, colors and shapes, font size and style. Proximity and spacing, similarity and dissimilarity. In some cases the biggest decision to get right is when to interpret data, and when to leave it up to the user to make a decision. The wrong graph can undo an otherwise great piece of work.

As you can probably tell, I'm not a graphic designer. What I am good at is working with a designer to refine a preliminary design, by thinking about the personas, the workflow, and how humans process information, and also what the capabilities of our tools are. A slightly 'better design' that takes much longer to implement and has ongoing costs associated with maintenance is no improvement at all. Better to look for bigger wins elsewhere.

What does "Full Stack" mean to me?

When I think 'Full Stack', or indeed when I'm looking for someone who labels themselves as full stack, I want them for one of two reasons. On a big or complex project, I need people who can understand the entire application from top to bottom. Someone who can reason about it, see failure modes and choke points to our success, and find concrete ways to improve the situation. Someone who can help design the information flow within the system, simplifying the system and opening up new avenues for future features. People who know one aspect of the problem can sit around saying that it's somebody else's responsibility to deal with it. A Full Stack person gets closer to the truth, and can offer multiple solutions.

On a smaller project, I may not actually know what mix of skills I need for a successful project. The mix may change from one version to the next, depending on the target audience. In this case my Full Stack developers are also a hedge against bottlenecks. They allow me to get my 'Truck Number' high without hiring a bunch of people I can't keep busy or would struggle to manage. I value someone who's willing to expand or learn a skill to support the success of the entire team. This week it might be setting up a build server. Next it might be upgrading a database. I might need extra hands to kick start a testing harness, or I might be behind on server side data validation. I need someone who can either pick up a new task, or can at least help out the other folks by doing their own troubleshooting.

When I say "I'm a full stack developer", these two areas are the qualities that I refer to.

My Skills and Experience

In addition to Human Factors, Analysis and Design, I have some small skill in system administration. But one of the places I feel I excel is in root cause analysis.

'Root Cause Analysis' is just a fancy way of saying "getting to the meat of a problem". It requires an ability to effectively identify and eliminate theories that might explain a problem. It requires a certain kind of devious mind, and a healthy dash of persistence. I fix performance problems when everyone else has thrown their hands up in defeat. I have worked on behalf of my employer with numerous vendors to identify bugs and performance issues from embedded systems like Aicas, to Wind River and a handful of Hardware Security Module manufacturers, to software and hardware vendors like F5.

I like to solve hard problems, and find ways to make the team more effective. I've maintained version control systems (once on my own dev machine), set up Continuous Integration environments and build pipelines for ten years. I've built developer tools, reusable libraries, instituted Process improvements, defined Code Review guidelines, established unit testing practices and spearheaded localization efforts. I've built authentication frameworks, code signing tools, reverse proxy configurations and Docker images. I've fixed more build scripts than I can rightly count, and it astounds me how consistently they possess the same set of bugs.
